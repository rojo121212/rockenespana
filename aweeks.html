Day 06

Today I woke up for my first day at Mujeres Supervivientes! A (not) wonderful Wednesday morning, I unfortunately did not get much sleep. After a breakfast of corn flakes and a chocolate crossaint with sprinkles, we headed to the community kitchen to make lunch. When I first arrived, I experienced for the first time, something I had only heard about prior: the traditional Spanish greetings. In Spanish culture, when you see a close friend, or at times meet new people, it is common to give the person a hug and two kisses, one on either cheek.
As I stated prior, I did not get much sleep the night before, and my brain was not functioning at full capacity. I saw two of my peers greet one of the other volunteers, as he hugged them and kissed them on each cheek. When it was my turn to greet him, in true American fashion, my first instinct was to shake his hand, which I did. I felt like I picked the wrong option in a game of episode. Anywho, the day went on, it was awesome to meet everyone and make new friends, but I quickly realized I needed to practice my Spanish. We chopped carrots, onions, and celery, and helped to make a pasta salad that we handed out to the community. Afterwards, we sat together and enjoyed the extra food, cafe, and sweets. We said goodbye to our new friends, and left to go to the market to exchange my pants.
Unfortunately, I had another mishap with the pants, but I returned home with my friends, hit the gym, and got some McDonalds. We went to our first seminar about Corporate Social Responsibility, and learned a lot about the direction we want to take our seminar in.

Day 07

Today is a holiday! Thurday in Spain is Corpus Christi, a holiday in which people flock to the streets of Sevilla to observe the celebrations that cross through and around the city center. We watched the crowds of families and people dressed in their best attire with almost 4 ft long candlesticks as they brought them down each street along with a moat carried by men with Jesus atop it. We explored more downtown after watching the celebrations, found a nice coffee shop, and went back to the residence.
Interestingly, there is not many sit down coffee shops, especially in the city center. Learning more and more about Sevilla through my experience, seminar, and talking with locals, I've learned how truly harsh the impact of tourism and gentrification is in Sevilla.
A double-edged sword, tourism in Sevilla brings lots to the city, it increases profits of spending that takes place, from hotels to restaurants to souvenir shops, but it seems there is more that the tourism takes away. From constant new construction to people pushed out of their homes, it has changed the city.
For example, the house buildings in the city center no longer house people. They were bought out and sold to hotel companies to hold tourists when they visited. Rather than allow the locals to continue to live there, developers buy out near entire blocks, raising the cost of living there and forcing local people to move out of their homes or to rent their homes out as air BNBs.
After this, I went to the gym for the first time in a while, which was nice. I met a couple other American students there, from Florida, who were studying law. As a crew, we went to work and plan our Granada trip in a coffee shop in the mall near our residence, and after that I got some ramen next door to remind me of home. Finishing off the day, I went to watch the sun set and went to bed.

Day 08

Friday - first day of work! And today we left for Granada.
As interns at Mujeres Supervivientes, we went to our first day of work and first activity. In the event I observed, members of The Indigenous People's Organization CAN-Jíbaro de Boriké visited and symbolically decolonized the Real Alcazar, one of the sites around Spain of Christopher Columbus. The group engaged in a group activity of looking through past history on an over 300-ft scroll together, and for the final activity, the group displayed a large banner that read “There was never a conquest, we continue in resistance” and sounded the guamo. It was powerful to have observed such an act, as it underscored the importance of history, of community, and of social justice. The expression of emotions after the event allowed us as interns to see the necessity of trust, care, and of vocalizing concerns. ,
